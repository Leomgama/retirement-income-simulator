The middle line is the most likely outcome. Half of the futures end up better than this, half worse.
The dark band is the likely range, where the middle half of all futures land. A typical result sits somewhere in here (in stats terms, the 25th to 75th out of 100).
The light band is a wider range that covers about 8 of every 10 futures (the 10th to 90th). Only the rare luckiest and unluckiest cases fall outside it.

How to read it: moving right, the bands spread apart with age because good and bad luck add up over the years. As long as the shaded area stays well above the bottom, the money holds up. Where the lower edge drops to the floor, those are the futures where the money runs out.

How this works and what it assumes

Each run plays out a fresh string of yearly market returns, takes out your spending, and grows what is left. Doing it 10,000 times shows how often the money survives, including the unlucky runs where weak markets hit early in retirement. Everything is in today's dollars, so spending stays flat in real terms.

Hypothetical illustration using simplified, long-run market assumptions. Not financial advice, a recommendation, or a guarantee of future results. Markets can behave outside these ranges.
